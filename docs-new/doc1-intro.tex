\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\noteCLAUDE{Three-sentence test: (1) two paradigms exist, (2) no common
language relates them, (3) this paper supplies one. Anchor for
everything that follows.}
Machine learning relies on two paradigms for reducing the cost of
large-scale convex optimization: feature screening, which eliminates
variables, and sample screening, which eliminates training points.
Both paradigms exploit convex duality, yet each has been derived in a
model-specific, case-by-case manner, and no common mathematical
abstraction has been established to relate them. This paper shows that
the two are Fenchel-dual manifestations of a single screening
operation, a claim established through a representation framework in
which screening decomposes into translation followed by restriction.

\noteCLAUDE{Screening is large including safe and unsafe approaches.}
Screening is a central technique for scaling convex optimization in
machine learning. Given a large-scale learning problem, a screening
rule identifies, before or during optimization, a subset of variables
or training samples that can be discarded without altering the
solution, reducing the size of the problem that must actually be
solved. Two paradigms have emerged from this idea. Feature screening
eliminates variables and originated in sparse learning models such as
the Lasso, where the optimal solution is known in advance to vanish on
many coordinates \citep{elghaoui2010safe}. Sample screening eliminates
training points and was developed later for kernel methods such as the
support vector machine, where inactive samples are identified through
dual certificates \citep{ogawa2014sample}. Subsequent work combined
the two paradigms into simultaneous screening rules
\citep{shibagaki2016simultaneous}, several of which are derived through
Fenchel--Rockafellar duality \citep{ndiaye2017gap,yamada2021dynamic,tran2025one}.
This paper does not survey these existing rules; it studies the
mathematical relationship between the two paradigms that produced
them.

\noteCLAUDE{This is the actual gap: shared reliance on duality, no shared
derivation. Everything before this was motivation; this is the claim a
reviewer could disagree with.}
This shared reliance on convex duality has not translated into a
shared derivation. Feature screening is typically derived from primal
sparsity structures, while sample screening is developed through dual
optimization formulations. As a result, each new screening rule is
established on a case-by-case basis, without a shared mathematical
vocabulary connecting the two paradigms. However, this similarity has
remained informal. Although the two paradigms appear to be dual, it
remains unclear in what mathematical sense this duality should be
understood.

\noteCLAUDE{This is the paper's only research question; nothing later
should read as a second, competing question.}
\begin{center}
\emph{Feature and sample
screening seems tobe dual, but how to formulize it?}
\end{center}

\noteCLAUDE{The paper's one "no, but" move: pre-empts "this was already
known informally" by naming formalization, not discovery, as the
contribution. Appears exactly once, here.}
The goal of this paper is not to argue that feature screening and
sample screening are dual. Rather, it is to formulate this apparent
duality as a precise mathematical statement.

This is the paper's only research question. Existing screening methods
are typically derived in a model-specific manner; our goal is not to
derive another screening rule, but to identify the common mathematical
structure underlying these derivations.

\noteCLAUDE{The one sentence a reader should remember, stated before any
framework is introduced.}
We introduce a common representation level on which both paradigms are
expressed as transformations of the same mathematical object, so that
Fenchel duality intertwines feature screening with sample screening.

\noteCLAUDE{Explains why the main insight is not obvious, with no formulas
yet: primal construction vs. dual construction look unrelated.}
This insight is not obvious because the two constructions look, on the
surface, unrelated. Feature screening fixes primal variables at a
candidate value; sample screening replaces loss terms by a
Fenchel--Young identity at a candidate dual point. One acts on primal
variables, the other on dual certificates, and a direct comparison
between them is not possible without a common language for both.

\noteCLAUDE{The framework is a proof strategy, not a second contribution;
keep this subordinate to the main insight above.}
To bridge this gap, we introduce a representation framework in which
every screening operator decomposes into translation followed by
restriction, and Translation Duality is the algebraic mechanism
connecting the two constructions. The framework exists only to make
the main insight provable rather than assumed; it is a proof strategy,
not a second contribution alongside it.
























\noteCLAUDE{Introduces the paper's one central object, the FR
representation, and its dual.}
Convex optimization problems of the form
\[
    \min_{x\in\mathbb R^n}
    f(Ax)+g(x)+a,
    \qquad
    A\in\mathbb R^{m\times n},
    \qquad
    a\in\mathbb R,
\]
with separable
\(
f(y)=\sum_{j=1}^m f_j(y_j)
\)
and
\(
g(x)=\sum_{i=1}^n g_i(x_i)
\),
are encoded as a single quadruple \(p=(f,g,A,a)\), an FR
representation. Columns of \(A\) are features; rows are samples.

\noteCLAUDE{Restates both screening constructions in the FR language; this
is what \emph{Why Is This Difficult?} needs so the two can be
compared at all.}
Feature screening, restated in this language, assumes every optimal
solution satisfies \(x_{\bar I}=z\) for a block
\(I\sqcup\bar I=\{1,\ldots,n\}\), which reduces the problem to
\[
    \min_{x_I}
    f(A_Ix_I+A_{\bar I}z)+g_I(x_I)+a+g_{\bar I}(z).
\]
Sample screening, restated in the same language, assumes every optimal
solution satisfies \(s\in\partial f_{\bar J}(A_{\bar J}x)\) for a block
\(J\sqcup\bar J=\{1,\ldots,m\}\); the Fenchel--Young equality
\(f_{\bar J}(A_{\bar J}x)=\langle s,A_{\bar J}x\rangle-f_{\bar J}^*(s)\)
then reduces the problem to
\[
    \min_x
    f_J(A_Jx)+g(x)+\langle A_{\bar J}^{\top}s,x\rangle+a-f_{\bar J}^*(s).
\]
Within our framework, the dual certificates used in sample screening
admit a natural interpretation as fixing supporting slopes via the
Fenchel--Young identity.\footnote{Throughout the introduction we write
the operators \(S^F, S^S, R^F, R^S, T^F, T^S\) without their screening
parameters, for readability.}

\noteCLAUDE{The paper's one central theorem, stated once here, not proved;
no other theorem should be introduced in the introduction. The diagram
is the interpretation, not the anchor: it comes last.}
The formulation promised above is the following identity.
\[
    (S^F(p))^*
    =
    S^S(p^*),
\]
where \(S^F\) denotes feature screening, \(S^S\) denotes sample
screening, and \((\cdot)^*\) denotes Fenchel--Rockafellar duality.
Feature screening on the primal representation is exactly sample
screening on the dual representation, which is exactly the commutative
diagram
\[
\begin{array}{ccc}
p & \xrightarrow{S^F} & S^F(p)\\
{\scriptstyle *}\big\downarrow & & \big\downarrow{\scriptstyle *}\\
p^* & \xrightarrow{S^S} & S^S(p^*)
\end{array}
\]

\noteCLAUDE{Names the theorem's significance before any secondary result;
consequences named here, detailed only in Section~\ref{sec:screening}.}
This identity establishes a structural correspondence between the two
screening paradigms. As a consequence, results developed for one
paradigm can be transferred to the other through duality. Intrinsic
complexity reduction and simultaneous screening both follow naturally
from this correspondence: they are what the theorem produces, not
separate reasons to care about it.

\noteCLAUDE{Three contributions, tiered to match the Result Hierarchy: a
framework, the main duality result, and its corollaries.}
This paper makes three contributions. The first is a representation
framework, comprising translation, restriction, and Fenchel
Equivariance, introduced only as the means to prove the second. The
second, and central, contribution is feature--sample duality, the
identity \((S^F(p))^*=S^S(p^*)\) stated above. The third comprises its
consequences, intrinsic complexity reduction and simultaneous
screening, both following from feature--sample duality.

\noteCLAUDE{Narrative only, not a contribution; positions safe screening as
future work built on this structural theory.}
The paper separates the structural theory of screening from the
semantic certificates required for safety. This provides a foundation
for future work on safe screening rules, where one additionally
imposes conditions ensuring that the screened problem preserves the
solution set of the original problem.

\noteCLAUDE{Previews Sections~\ref{sec:operators} and~\ref{sec:screening}
without claiming results they haven't proved yet.}
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:operators}
introduces the primitive translation and restriction operations on
Fenchel--Rockafellar representations and proves the Fenchel
Equivariance theorem. Section~\ref{sec:screening} defines feature
screening and sample screening from optimality assumptions,
immediately states the intrinsic complexity corollary, proves they
decompose as translation followed by restriction, proves they are
Fenchel-dual, the paper's main theorem, and immediately states the
simultaneous screening corollary. The final section discusses how the
framework can be extended toward safe screening rules by adding
suitable safety certificates.
